The Monster of Mankind

1954 was a landmark year for Japanese cinema, marked by two gigantic productions from Toho that would change cinema forever. The dual production of Akira Kurosawa's Seven Samurai and Ishiro Honda's Godzilla nearly bankrupted the company, but the huge domestic and international success of both films forever put Japanese cinema on the map as a force to be reckoned with. While Seven Samurai was an epic set in times long past, Godzilla was a terrifying drama set in contemporary post-war Japan.

While later Godzilla and monster films were made purely as entertainment in a fantasy version of our world, this was not the case for the original Godzilla film. Made only nine years after the atomic bombs detonated over Hiroshima and Nagasaki, with the near-apocalyptic levels of destruction from both still fresh in everyone's memory, the movie absolutely terrified Japanese moviegoers with its image of Tokyo ravaged by an uncontrolled monster, created by mankind's hubris regarding nuclear technology.

As we watch Godzilla wreak destruction upon Japan, the film forces us to face the question of who is the biggest monster: the creature itself or mankind, who created it? As something as rare as a huge blockbuster that poses existential questions about the dangers of nuclear weapons, Godzilla remains a must-see classic for all lovers of great cinema.
